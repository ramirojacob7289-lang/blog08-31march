5 Communication Habits of Strong Couples

Research-backed communication techniques that strengthen relationships. Transform conflict into connection and understanding.

Dr. Amanda Foster
Relationship Therapist
March 10, 2026 • 7 min read

After studying thousands of couples over four decades, relationship researcher Dr. John Gottman can predict with 94% accuracy whether a couple will divorce—just by observing how they communicate for 15 minutes.

The good news? Strong communication isn't about never arguing. It's about how you argue, how you repair, and how you connect daily. These five research-backed habits distinguish couples who thrive from those who struggle.

1. They Practice Active Listening (Really Listening)

Most people don't listen to understand—they listen to respond. Strong couples do the opposite. When your partner shares something important, put down your phone, make eye contact, and listen with the goal of understanding their perspective, not formulating your rebuttal.

The technique: After your partner finishes speaking, summarize what you heard before responding: "What I'm hearing is that you felt frustrated when I didn't text you about being late. Is that right?" This simple validation makes your partner feel heard and often de-escalates tension.

2. They Use "I" Statements Instead of "You" Accusations

The fastest way to start a fight? Begin sentences with "You always..." or "You never..." These accusations trigger defensiveness and escalate conflict. Strong couples frame concerns from their own perspective.

Instead of: "You never help with housework!"
Try: "I feel overwhelmed when I'm doing all the cleaning. Can we work out a plan together?"

Notice the difference? The first blames. The second expresses feelings and invites collaboration. "I" statements own your emotions without attacking your partner.

3. They Turn Toward Bids for Connection

Gottman's research found that happy couples turn toward each other's "bids for connection" 86% of the time, while couples headed for divorce only did so 33% of the time. What's a bid for connection?

It's any attempt to get attention, affection, or conversation—from "Look at this funny meme" to "How was your day?" These seemingly small moments are the building blocks of emotional intimacy.

Turning toward: Putting down your phone, making eye contact, engaging genuinely
Turning away: Ignoring, giving one-word answers, staying focused on your screen

Responding to these micro-moments throughout the day creates a positive emotional bank account. When conflict arises, you'll have goodwill to draw from.

4. They Repair During and After Conflicts

Here's a secret: All couples fight. What matters is how effectively they repair. Strong couples use "repair attempts"—words or actions that prevent negativity from escalating.

Effective repair attempts:

  • "I'm sorry, that came out wrong. Let me try again."
  • "Can we take a break? I need 20 minutes to cool down."
  • Using humor (when appropriate): "Okay, this is getting ridiculous. Can we reset?"
  • Physical touch: Reaching for their hand during a tense moment
  • "I love you, and I don't want to fight about this anymore."

The key is that both partners must recognize and accept repair attempts. If one person tries to de-escalate but the other dismisses it, the repair fails.

5. They Express Appreciation and Gratitude Regularly

The ratio matters: Gottman found that stable relationships have a 5:1 ratio of positive to negative interactions. For every criticism or complaint, you need five positive comments, gestures, or moments.

Strong couples actively look for things to appreciate:

  • "Thank you for making coffee this morning. That was really thoughtful."
  • "I appreciate how patient you were with my mother today."
  • "I noticed you've been working really hard lately. I see you."

These aren't grand gestures—they're small, specific acknowledgments. They combat the human tendency to take our partners for granted and remind both people why they chose each other.

Putting It Into Practice

Weekly Relationship Rituals

  • Daily check-ins: 10 minutes of phone-free conversation about your days
  • Weekly date nights: Prioritize uninterrupted time together
  • Monthly relationship meetings: Discuss what's working and what needs adjustment
  • Appreciation practice: Share three things you appreciate about each other weekly
  • Conflict cool-down protocol: Agree on a signal for taking breaks during heated discussions

Communication habits are exactly that—habits. They require practice and consistency. You won't get it perfect every time, and that's okay. What matters is your commitment to improving and repairing when you fall short.

Start with one habit from this list. Practice it intentionally for a month. Notice what changes. Then add another. Over time, these small shifts create profound transformations in how you connect, understand each other, and navigate challenges together.

Strong relationships aren't built on perfection—they're built on effective communication, genuine repair, and daily choices to turn toward each other with love and attention.
